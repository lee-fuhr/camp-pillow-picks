How to choose a camp pillow

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best camp pillow for you depends on your sleeping position, pack-size preferences, and budget. For side sleepers, look for a thicker, supportive foam or hybrid style; back and stomach sleepers can get away with a lower-profile inflatable. Consider a pillow with a soft, grippy cover to prevent sliding off your sleeping pad.

What to look for in a camp pillow

Start with three core factors: weight, packed size, and comfort. Inflatable pillows are the lightest and most packable, some fold down to the size of a fist, but they can feel crinkly or unstable. Foam pillows are heavier and bulkier but offer a more plush, familiar feel. Hybrid designs combine a thin inflatable core with a foam topper or a soft shell. Beyond those basics, pay attention to the valve. A simple push-button valve is easier to inflate and deflate than a twist-style one. A textured or brushed surface (often microfleece or brushed polyester) helps keep the pillow from sliding off an air pad. Many campers also appreciate a pillow with a built-in stuff sack that doubles as a compression bag.

Inflatable vs foam vs hybrid: which is best for your sleep style?

Pure inflatable pillows are great for weight-conscious backpackers. Most models weigh a few ounces and pack very small, but they offer little lateral support, side sleepers often wake up with the pillow squished flat. Foam pillows (often shredded memory foam or polyfill) are better for car camping or if you have room in your pack. They retain shape well and feel like a home pillow, but they’re noticeably heavier and bulkier. Hybrid pillows try to bridge the gap. A common design uses a thin inflatable bladder topped with a layer of foam or a soft fabric overlay. You can adjust firmness by adding or releasing air while still getting some cushioning. These hybrids are becoming popular for thru-hikers who prioritize comfort but still want a small packed size. Reviews on models like the Sea to Summit Aeros Premium or the Nemo Fillo consistently note that the hybrid approach reduces the &#8220;bouncy&#8221; feel of a pure air pillow.

How to match pillow size and loft to your sleeping position

Your sleeping position dictates how much lift you need from a pillow. Side sleepers need a taller loft, usually around 4 to 6 inches, to keep the spine aligned. A low-profile inflatable will leave your neck angled. For this group, a foam or hybrid pillow with at least moderate thickness is often the best choice. Back sleepers can use a medium loft (2 to 4 inches), and stomach sleepers generally want the lowest possible loft, sometimes just a thin stuff sack or a very flat inflatable. Length also matters. Most camp pillows are rectangular and roughly the size of a standard bed pillow, but some are square or shorter. If you move around a lot at night, a wider pillow gives you room to roll. For ultralight setups, a small, neck-sized pillow may suffice, but be prepared for it to shift off your pad if the surface is slick.

What about pillow covers and comfort features?

A removable, washable cover is a huge plus. Many camp pillows have a fixed fabric shell that can only be spot-cleaned, and over time sweat and oils build up. Pillows with a zip-off cover (like the Therm-a-Rest Compressible or the REI Co-op Trailbreak) can be thrown in a washing machine and dry quickly. Other comfort touches include a contoured shape that cradles the head, a built-in pillowcase pocket where you can stuff a jacket for extra height, or a non-slip backing coated with silicone dots. A few pillows use a &#8220;self-inflating&#8220; foam core that expands when you open the valve, though these are heavier than manual inflatables. Look for these features if you plan to use the pillow for many nights in a row.

How much should you spend on a camp pillow?

Prices vary from budget-friendly to premium. At the lower end, simple inflatable pillows can be very affordable; they work for occasional car camping but may not last a full season of regular use. Mid-range options (often around the &#8220;few tens of dollars&#8221; mark) include better valves, thicker fabrics, and removable covers. Many backpackers find that the investment in a mid-range hybrid or foam pillow pays off in sleep quality. At the high end, you get ultralight materials, advanced valve systems, and proprietary foam blends. These pillows can approach or exceed the cost of a home pillow. The extra money typically buys weight savings, quieter fabrics (less crinkle), and longer durability. It’s worth noting that a pillow you actually use and sleep well on is almost always a better value than a cheap pillow you replace every trip.

How to maintain and clean your camp pillow

For inflatable pillows, the most common issue is a slow leak at the valve or seam. Always store the pillow partially inflated to keep the valve from getting squashed. If you get a pinhole, a patch kit for sleeping pads works; some brands sell dedicated repair patches. For foam pillows, air them out after each trip to prevent mildew. A few hours in the sun or a spin in a large mesh bag helps fluff up compressed foam. Many campers worry about the pillow absorbing sweat odors. A washable cover is your best defense; if the pillow itself is machine-washable, use a gentle cycle with mild soap and tumble dry on low. Avoid fabric softeners, which can reduce breathability and cling to the fabric. With basic care, a good camp pillow should last for several seasons.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a regular pillow for camping?

You can, but it’s rarely worth the space. A standard bed pillow takes up a huge amount of room in a pack and offers little insulation from the cold ground. It also gets damp easily from condensation. If you’re car camping with a large tent, a regular pillow is fine; for backpacking, use a dedicated camp pillow.

How do I keep an inflatable pillow from sliding off my sleeping pad?

Look for a pillow with a textured or brushed bottom surface. Many models have a &#8220;gripper&#8221; coating or silicone dots. You can also place a thin towel or your sleeping bag’s hood underneath the pillow, or use a pillow retention sleeve sewn into some sleeping bag hoods.

Are camp pillows really worth it?

For most people, yes. After a long day of hiking, a good night&#8217;s sleep makes a huge difference in energy and morale. Using a stuff sack or pile of clothes often leads to neck pain and restless sleep. A properly chosen camp pillow improves spinal alignment and comfort, and the weight penalty is minimal&#8212;often just a few ounces.

What's the difference between a pillow and a pillowcase?

A camp pillow is the cushion itself; a pillowcase is a removable fabric cover that protects the pillow and adds softness. Some pillows come with an integrated case, while others require a separate purchase. A good pillowcase made of fleece or brushed polyester can make a basic inflatable feel much nicer against the skin.

How do I fix a leak in an inflatable camp pillow?

First, inflate the pillow and submerge it in water to find the bubbles. Mark the hole, dry the area completely, and apply a patch from a standard sleeping pad repair kit (like Tenacious Tape or a vinyl patch). Most inflatable pillows use the same materials as sleeping pads. For valve leaks, check that the gasket is seated properly or try a tiny dab of silicone grease.

Can I use a stuff sack as a camp pillow?

It’s a common lightweight backup, but it’s rarely comfortable. Fill the sack with soft clothing or a puffy jacket and place it under your head. The result is often lumpy and unstable. For a few ounces more, a dedicated inflatable pillow offers much better support and a flatter sleeping surface.

In shortChoosing a camp pillow is about matching your sleep style, pack space, and comfort needs. Inflatable models work for ultralight trips, foam for car camping, and hybrids for the best of both worlds. Pay attention to loft, cover fabric, and valve quality, and your pillow will help you actually rest on the trail. Don&#8217;t overthink it&#8212;any real pillow is better than a wad of clothes.
