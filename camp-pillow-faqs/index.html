Camp pillow questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best camp pillow for you balances packed size, comfort, and price – it’s not just about softness. Inflatable pillows save space but can feel plasticky; compressible pillows mimic your bed pillow but take up more room. Think about how you sleep and what you’re willing to carry before you buy.

What are the main types of camp pillows?

There are three broad categories: inflatable pillows, compressible (foam or down-filled) pillows, and stuff-sack hybrid pillows. Inflatable pillows pack incredibly small – about the size of a fist – and weigh next to nothing, but they can feel like a balloon and require you to blow them up. Compressible pillows use shredded foam or synthetic fill and feel much closer to your bed pillow at home, but they take up as much space as a small loaf of bread and weigh more. Stuff-sack pillows are essentially a pouch with soft fabric that you fill with your spare clothing; they’re free if you already have a jacket, but they shift around and aren’t very consistent.

How should I choose between weight and comfort?

If you’re backpacking and every gram counts, an inflatable pillow is your best bet – most weigh under a few ounces and pack smaller than a soda can. For car camping or shorter trips where weight isn’t critical, a compressible pillow will give you a far more restful sleep, especially if you’re a side sleeper. Consider your sleep position too: back sleepers can often get by with a lower-profile inflatable, while side sleepers need more loft and often prefer a thicker compressible pillow or a tall inflatable with a textured surface.

What features make a camp pillow more comfortable?

Look for a contoured shape or a dip in the center that cradles your head, which helps keep your spine aligned. Surface texture matters a lot: some inflatable pillows have a micro-suede or brushed fabric top that feels less sticky and noisy than bare plastic. For compressible pillows, the type of fill affects comfort – shredded memory foam molds better than loose fiberfill. Also, check whether the pillow has a separate adjustment chamber or a two-way valve that lets you fine-tune firmness. A removable, washable cover is a huge plus for hygiene after a few dusty trips.

Are camp pillows difficult to clean and maintain?

Most inflatable pillows can be wiped down with mild soap and water after you deflate them; rinse the valve carefully to avoid clogging. Compressible pillows with removable covers are easiest – just unzip and machine-wash the cover on gentle, then air dry. If the fill gets smelly, you can spot-clean with a damp cloth and a little detergent, but avoid submerging the entire pillow because foam holds moisture and can develop mildew. Store all pillows fully dry and, for inflatables, with a tiny bit of air left inside to keep the plastic from sticking together.

Can I use a regular pillow for camping?

You certainly can, but there are trade-offs. A standard bed pillow takes up a lot of space and provides no insulation from the cold ground – it will soak up moisture from your tent floor quickly. If you car-camp and have room, bring your favorite pillow, but put it inside a dry bag or a waterproof stuff sack to keep it clean and dry. For backpacking, a regular pillow is impractical because of its bulk and weight. A better middle ground is a dedicated camp pillow that compresses or inflates to a fraction of the size.

What should I know about inflatable pillow valves?

Valves vary a lot: screw valves are slow to inflate but seal reliably, while push-pull valves let you adjust firmness on the fly but can slowly leak if not seated fully. Some pillows use a one-way flap valve that works well but makes deflating a hassle – you have to squeeze the air out through the same tiny opening. Look for a valve that allows both easy inflation and quick deflation, ideally with a wide opening so you’re not huffing and puffing for minutes. A valve cap that doubles as a mini pump is a neat bonus but not essential.

Frequently asked questions

How do I fix a leaky inflatable camp pillow?

Find the hole by inflating the pillow and submerging it in water – look for bubbles. Dry the area, clean it with alcohol, and apply a patch from a repair kit designed for outdoor gear. Most pillows come with a patch, but generic tent-patch material works too. Let the adhesive cure for the time stated on the package before using it again.

Are down-filled camp pillows worth the extra money?

Down-filled compressible pillows are lighter and pack smaller than foam-filled ones, and they feel very luxurious. The downsides are cost, poor performance when wet, and the need to keep them dry. If you camp in dry climates or are okay with using a waterproof stuff sack, a down pillow can be a great splurge.

Can I use a camping pillow for side sleeping?

Yes, but you need enough loft and firmness. Look for a compressible pillow that is at least a few inches thick when laid out, or an inflatable model with a built-in chamber that allows you to add more air. Some brands make tall or &#8220;wedge&#8221; designs specifically for side sleepers. If the pillow flattens under your head, your neck will be strained.

How do I store a camp pillow when not in use?

For inflatable pillows, deflate completely, roll gently to push out remaining air, and store in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight. For compressible pillows, store them uncompressed or loosely rolled to keep the fill from losing its loft. Never store any pillow while damp &#8211; that’s how mold starts.

What’s the best camp pillow for someone who sleeps on their stomach?

Stomach sleepers need the thinnest pillow possible to avoid craning the neck. A low-profile inflatable pillow with minimal fill works best, or even a flat stuff-sack pillow. Some brands make extra-low inflatables, and you can always partially inflate a standard model to get the right height.

In shortA good camp pillow doesn’t have to cost a lot, but it should match your sleep style and the type of camping you do. Take a few minutes to think about your priorities – packed size, comfort, durability – and read reviews from people who have used the pillow on real trips. Your neck will thank you in the morning.
